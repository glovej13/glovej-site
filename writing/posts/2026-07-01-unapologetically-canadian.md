I moved to Germany when I was two months old. I can't count it — I have no memory of the place, and I was there under different circumstances. I've lived in four provinces since, spent real time in a couple of other countries, and done enough travelling to have a frame of reference. So when I say I wouldn't want to live anywhere else, it's not a default. It's a considered position.

Every year at this time I stop and actually think about that. Canada Day tends to do it. This year, something else helped. CTV ran a promo before Sunday's World Cup match featuring Jeremy Hansen — the Canadian astronaut who flew around the moon on Artemis II back in April — and it stopped me in a way I didn't expect. If you haven't seen it, it's worth two minutes: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rpYUROzq-gw

The video is a montage of Canadian athletes and moments: Terry Fox. Paul Henderson in '72. Crosby's golden goal in Vancouver. Christine Sinclair. Shai Gilgeous-Alexander. Mike Weir. Steve Nash. Andre De Grasse. People who went somewhere unfamiliar, in disciplines and arenas this country doesn't always back loudly enough, and came back having done something worth remembering. Terry Fox deserves more than a passing mention here — Sunday was the 45th anniversary of his passing, and the man ran a marathon a day on one leg across this country to raise money for cancer research before his body gave out. He didn't make it across. The run did. That's not inspiration poster stuff. That's what the thing actually looks like.

Hansen's point, over all of it: being humble shouldn't stop us from setting big goals and chasing big dreams. Set the goal. Expect the challenge. Allow for failure and adaptation. Persevere until you get there. He framed it as what Canadians actually are at their best — and I think he's right, even if we don't say it that way about ourselves.

I'm not a soccer fan. I watched Canada's games this tournament anyway, because there's something about your country competing on home soil in a sport it's never dominated that cuts through the usual reflex — the one that says *let's not get ahead of ourselves*. That's the same feeling I get at the Olympics, watching Canadians compete in speed skating, gymnastics, track — disciplines the country barely registers for the other four years at a stretch — and suddenly the whole country has something to care about. It's not tribalism. It's closer to recognizing your people somewhere unexpected.

This connects to something bigger than sport right now, and I'll say it plainly. My generation was largely taught to keep politics out of writing like this. I'm done with that particular piece of received wisdom. What Mark Carney is doing — building genuine international respect, positioning Canada toward its own economic footing, refusing the passive dependency — is the same dare-to-dream-big energy Hansen is describing. And it's not just positioning. Canada's AI policy work is a real example of this: we're making deliberate choices about how we show up in that space rather than just absorbing whatever arrives from the south. I write about this on LinkedIn regularly. The point here is that Canada is claiming a seat at tables it used to be too polite to demand, and the world is starting to treat that seriously.

The hockey thing. I'll say it. The men's national program is a mess, and the mythology around it doesn't help anything. I coached both my sons. I've spent enough years inside minor hockey to have earned an opinion. You can love the sport — love what Henderson meant in '72, what Crosby's goal meant to anyone who was watching — and still be honest about where things stand right now. Those aren't in conflict. Loving something enough to criticize it honestly is part of what unapologetic actually means.

On culture: there's a lot I genuinely respect about what European countries have built around the parts of life that aren't work. Real time off. Meals that take as long as they take. A cultural investment in the individual and the family that runs deeper than a policy document. Canada has some of that in its DNA. What I'm less comfortable with is the gravitational pull from the other direction — the US corporate framing, the always-on assumptions, the quiet suggestion that your worth is a function of your output and your output alone. We absorb more of it than we notice. Proximity does that. Recognizing it is the first step to something better.

What actually makes this country different is harder to name than people expect, because it's not a policy or a bumper sticker. It's more like: you can be a lot of different things here — where you're from, who you love, how you show up in the world — and still have the maple leaf mean something to you. Not because we've erased the differences. Because somewhere along the way we decided to pull everyone under the same blanket and call it ours.

That's not finished work, and we have a lot left to do. But I see a lot of people trying, and that's where it matters — how we show up for each other every day, how we take the time to understand what others are feeling and living through, how we keep moving forward in ways that bring us together without forcing anyone to change who they are. It's a hard balance. We all need to keep doing it.

If you want to see what Canadian identity looks like when you pull it out of the abstract and put it in a longbox — I collect Team Canada hockey cards and I share that every year, but this Canada Day I went deeper. Check out my other Canada Day post on this site that explores my Collecting Canada themes.

If the Hansen video gives you the same feeling the I Am Canadian rant did the first time you heard it — but with actual stakes, and at a moment when it matters more — that's exactly where I'm at.

Happy Canada Day.
