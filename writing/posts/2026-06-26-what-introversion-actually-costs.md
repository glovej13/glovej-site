I would rather stand in front of 200 people and give a presentation than walk into a cocktail hour with 50.

Most people find that hard to believe. Public speaking is the thing everyone's afraid of. Small talk is just small talk.

But I learned something in an HR workshop years ago that made it make sense: introverts spend energy being around people. Extroverts gain it from a room. The cocktail hour isn't uncomfortable the way stage fright is uncomfortable — it's draining in a way that's harder to explain. I'll survive it. I'll even be glad I did it. But I'll be exhausted when I leave, and I was nervous walking in.

That's what introversion actually costs. Not the ability to show up — the energy it takes to do it.

The second cost is harder to name. A lot of what goes unsaid isn't purely about being an introvert. Some of it is about the content itself. For me it's the geek side of my life — cards, comics, the whole collecting world I've been in since I was a kid. Not cool at the time. You learn quickly which things are worth explaining and which ones you just quietly set aside. Societal norms have a way of keeping people quiet about things that, honestly, most of us would admit are pretty harmless. Mine happened to be sci-fi and sports cards. For someone else it's something else entirely. The thing being kept quiet changes. The math doesn't.

What I've come to believe is that the veneer costs more than whatever it's hiding. When you're carrying something you don't need to carry, it takes up space — and it makes it harder for the people around you to actually see you. You become a version of yourself instead of yourself. That's true for the harmless stuff — the hobbies, the interests — and it's equally true for the harder stuff. The feelings men especially don't tend to say out loud. The job loss. The self-doubt that follows. I've known people who held things in their whole lives and the people closest to them had no idea what was really going on. That matters. Being readable to the people who care about you matters.

I turned 50 and got laid off in the same year. Both things have a way of clarifying what you've been putting off. I built this site because I needed somewhere to say things I'd been sitting on — collecting, yes, but also the harder stuff. The introversion is a big part of why it took this long. So is the collecting stigma, if I'm being honest. The things you kept quiet when you were young because they weren't cool — there's usually nothing wrong with them. You just needed to be old enough not to care.

Writing this was the hard version. The site existing is the harder version. This is the explanation for both.

That's the explanation. Now we can get into the rest of it.
